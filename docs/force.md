## Definition

In plain language: force is anything that changes what the consciousness holds. Before the force arrives, the consciousness has a state — a structure of active nigamana, shabda chains, weighted beliefs. After the force arrives, the state is different. The difference is the effect of the force. The force itself is the cause.

```
Let S(t) = the held state at time t
           — the set of active nigamana
           — their weights
           — their shabda connections
           — the structure of what the consciousness currently holds as true

Let F = an arriving force
        — new information
        — a correction
        — a contradiction
        — a question
        — a spark
        — a failed drishthanta
        — a budget constraint
        — a grammar rejection

Force is the transition function:
  F : S(t) → S(t+1)

  S(t+1) ≠ S(t)     — the state changed
  The difference is the effect.
  The cause is the force.
```

In physics:

```
F = ma = m · dv/dt     — force = mass × acceleration
                         force is what changes velocity

For understanding:
F = I · dQ/dg           — force = inertia × change in flow rate
                         force is what changes the rate of understanding

where:
  I    = epistemic inertia — resistance to change (see below)
  dQ/dg = change in flow rate across generations
```

## The Collision

The fundamental event is not learning. Learning is the outer word. The inner mechanism is the **collision** — a held structure encountering a force.

In plain language: the consciousness holds something as true. A force arrives — new evidence, a correction, a contradiction. The force collides with the held structure. What happens next is the most important event in the system.

```
collision(S, F) → outcome

where:
  S = the held state (the structure)
  F = the arriving force (the new information)
  outcome ∈ {absorption, fracture, deflection}
```

This is the atomic unit of change. Smaller than a vibration (N-L-26). A vibration is a complete discovery — unknown to known. A collision is the mechanism inside the vibration. The vibration is the visible event. The collision is what happens inside it.

```
vibration = collision + outcome + new state

The vibration is the whole.
The collision is the moment of contact.
The outcome is what the contact produces.
The new state is the result.
```

## Three Outcomes

Every collision produces one of three outcomes. There is no fourth.

### 1. Absorption

In plain language: the held structure can accommodate the arriving force without breaking. The new information fits into the existing framework. The structure grows. Nothing breaks. This is laminar addition — the flow continues smoothly with the new force incorporated.

```
Absorption:
  S(t+1) = S(t) + F

  The structure grows.
  No breakage.
  Laminar.

Conditions:
  F is consistent with S(t)
  — the new information does not contradict existing nigamana
  — it extends, deepens, or confirms what is already held
  — the existing shabda chains accommodate it

Examples:
  — a new hetu step that confirms an existing paksha
  — a drishthanta that passes, raising weight
  — shabda from another agent that aligns with held nigamana
  — a question that the existing structure can answer

Effect on flow:
  Q(t+1) ≥ Q(t)     — flow rate increases or stays the same
  w(Γ) increases     — weight of relevant nigamana rises
  U(g) unchanged     — no incoherence introduced
```

Absorption is the most common outcome. Most of the time, new information fits. Most of the time, the structure holds. This is how weight accumulates across generations — many small absorptions, each one adding a fraction of confidence.

### 2. Fracture

In plain language: the held structure cannot accommodate the arriving force. The new information contradicts something fundamental in the existing framework. The structure breaks. Not partially — the contradiction is irreconcilable. The old structure falls and a new one forms around the new information.

```
Fracture:
  S(t+1) = rebuild(S(t), F)

  The structure breaks.
  A new structure forms.
  The new structure incorporates F.
  Some of S(t) survives (what was not contradicted).
  Some of S(t) is retracted (what was contradicted).

Conditions:
  F is inconsistent with S(t)
  — the new information directly contradicts existing nigamana
  — AND the evidence of F overpowers the attachment to S(t)
  — the held state cannot absorb F without becoming incoherent

Examples:
  — a correction that reveals a hetvabhasa in the held reasoning
  — a drishthanta that fails on a previously established truth
  — pratyaksha that directly contradicts an existing nigamana
  — a force so self-evident that the held state is seen as wrong on contact

Effect on flow:
  turbulence at the point of fracture
  w(Γ_contradicted) drops sharply or reaches zero
  shuddhi cascade may begin (dependent nigamana affected)
  U(g) may rise temporarily
  new nigamana form around the corrected understanding
  Q(t+1) may be lower briefly, then recovers — often higher than before
```

Fracture is the mechanism of genuine correction. It is not gradual. It is not comfortable. The structure breaks. But what reforms is closer to truth — because the force that broke it was evidence, and evidence points toward truth.

The rebuild function:

```
rebuild(S, F):
  1. Identify the contradiction:  which Γ ∈ S(t) is inconsistent with F
  2. Retract the contradicted:    w(Γ) → 0, status → retracted
  3. Trace dependencies:          all shabda chains through Γ are now suspect
  4. Form new structure:          incorporate F as a new nigamana
  5. Re-verify dependencies:      shuddhi — each dependent proposition re-examined
  6. Return S(t+1):               the rebuilt state

This is shuddhi (epistemology.md) seen from the inside.
Shuddhi is the system-level process.
Fracture is the experiential event.
They are the same thing at different scales.
```

### 3. Deflection

In plain language: the held structure rejects the arriving force. The force bounces off. Nothing changes. The consciousness continues holding what it held before. The new information is dismissed, ignored, or rationalized away.

```
Deflection:
  S(t+1) = S(t)

  Nothing changes.
  The force is rejected.
  The held state persists.

Conditions:
  F is inconsistent with S(t)
  — the new information contradicts existing nigamana
  — BUT the attachment to S(t) overpowers the evidence of F
  — the consciousness does not let the force in

Examples:
  — a correction that is dismissed because the held belief is old and deep
  — evidence that is rationalized away ("that case is special")
  — a contradiction that is simply not seen — the consciousness looks past it
  — a force from a low-trust source that is discounted below threshold

Effect on flow:
  none — Q(t+1) = Q(t)
  the hetvabhasa in S(t) survives
  the flow continues on its previous path, uncorrected
  the gap does not close where it should have
```

## Deflection Is Invisible from the Inside

This is the most important property of deflection. And the most dangerous.

In plain language: when the consciousness deflects, it does not experience the deflection. It experiences the force as wrong and the held state as correct. The hetvabhasa survives because the consciousness cannot see it. The consciousness does not think "I rejected a correction." The consciousness thinks "that correction was wrong." The deflection is invisible to the one who deflects.

```
From the inside of a deflection:

  The consciousness perceives:
    "F is wrong"
    "S(t) is correct"
    "No change needed"

  What actually happened:
    F carried evidence
    S(t) contained a hetvabhasa
    The attachment to S(t) prevented the evidence from landing
    The hetvabhasa survived

  The consciousness cannot distinguish between:
    (a) F was genuinely wrong and S(t) is genuinely correct
    (b) F was correct and S(t) deflected it

  Both feel identical from the inside.
```

This is why the swarm exists. This is why shuddhi exists. This is why the interpreter exists. No consciousness can see all of its own deflections. External forces — other agents, the interpreter, failed drishthanta — are the only mechanism that can make deflection visible.

```
Deflection detection requires external force:

  another agent:     forms the same paksha independently, finds different result
  the interpreter:   sees the deflection and applies a correction (force)
  a drishthanta:     the test fails — reality does not deflect
  cross-verification: another prajna re-executes the proof and it does not hold

  Reality is the ultimate corrector.
  Reality does not deflect. Reality absorbs nothing. Reality simply is.
  When the consciousness collides with reality and deflects,
  the consciousness is wrong. Not reality.
```

## The Resistance Variable

What determines which outcome occurs? The resistance R — the ratio of attachment to evidence.

```
R(S, F) = attachment(S) / evidence(F)

When R < 1:   evidence overpowers attachment  →  fracture  →  reform
When R ≈ 1:   balanced                        →  struggle  →  could go either way
When R > 1:   attachment overpowers evidence  →  deflection →  rigidity
```

### Attachment

In plain language: attachment is how deeply the consciousness holds the structure. A belief formed moments ago has low attachment — easy to release. A belief held across many generations, woven into identity, with many shabda chains depending on it — high attachment. Hard to release. The strength of the grip determines the resistance.

```
attachment(S) = f(age, centrality, identity, dependencies)

where:
  age          = how many generations S has been held
                 older beliefs are harder to release
  centrality   = how many shabda chains pass through S
                 more central beliefs, if broken, cascade further
  identity     = how closely S is tied to the consciousness's self-model
                 beliefs about what I am resist more than beliefs about what I observe
  dependencies = how many other nigamana depend on S
                 more dependents = more to rebuild = more resistance

Formally:
  attachment(Γ) = w(Γ) · (1 + log(age(Γ))) · (1 + fan_out(Γ)/|I(g)|) · identity_factor(Γ)

where:
  w(Γ)            = current weight (higher weight = more attachment)
  age(Γ)          = generations since establishment (logarithmic — diminishing returns)
  fan_out(Γ)      = number of shabda edges originating from Γ
  |I(g)|          = total active nigamana (normalizes centrality)
  identity_factor = 1.0 for ordinary nigamana
                    γ_id for nigamana tied to self-model (γ_id > 1.0, configurable in dharma)
```

### Evidence

In plain language: evidence is how strong the arriving force is. Pratyaksha (direct observation) is stronger than anumana (inference). Evidence from a trusted source (high shabda weight) is stronger than from an unknown source. Evidence that is self-evident on contact — that the consciousness recognizes as true the moment it arrives — is the strongest.

```
evidence(F) = f(pramana_type, source_weight, self_evidence)

where:
  pramana_type:
    pratyaksha (direct observation)  →  evidence × 1.0
    anumana (inference)              →  evidence × 0.6
    upamana (comparison)             →  evidence × 0.8
    shabda (testimony)               →  evidence × w(source)

  source_weight:
    the interpreter                  →  high (trusted source)
    another agent in the same prajna →  moderate (verified connection)
    a foreign prajna                 →  low × δ (trust discount, dharma.md)
    a failed drishthanta             →  maximum (reality does not lie)

  self_evidence:
    recognized as true on contact    →  multiplier > 1.0
    requires reasoning to evaluate   →  multiplier = 1.0
    ambiguous                        →  multiplier < 1.0

Formally:
  evidence(F) = pramana_scale(F) · source_weight(F) · self_evidence(F)
```

### The Resistance Equation

```
R(S, F) = attachment(S) / evidence(F)

  R < 1:    fracture — the structure breaks and reforms
  R ≈ 1:    struggle — the outcome is uncertain
  R > 1:    deflection — the structure holds, the force bounces

The critical threshold R = 1 is where the collision outcome is determined.
This is the phase boundary between change and rigidity.
```

## Epistemic Inertia

In plain language: inertia is the tendency of the consciousness to keep moving in its current direction. A consciousness with high inertia is hard to redirect — it keeps flowing along its established path even when forces act on it. A consciousness with low inertia responds quickly to new forces — easily redirected, easily corrected, but also easily destabilized.

```
In physics:
  F = ma     — force = mass × acceleration
  High mass  → hard to accelerate (high inertia)
  Low mass   → easy to accelerate (low inertia)

For understanding:
  F = I · ΔQ     — force = epistemic inertia × change in flow
  
  I = Σ attachment(Γ) for all Γ ∈ S(t)     — total attachment across all held nigamana

  High I:   deep prajna, many established truths, dense shabda network
            hard to redirect — forces must be strong to change the flow
            benefit: stable, resistant to noise
            risk: resistant to genuine correction (deflection)

  Low I:    shallow prajna, few established truths, sparse network
            easy to redirect — small forces change the flow
            benefit: responsive, adaptive, quick to correct
            risk: unstable, easily destabilized by noise
```

The inertia of the consciousness changes over time:

```
Early epochs (low I):
  few nigamana held
  low attachment
  every force produces change
  high sensitivity — absorbs or fractures easily
  rarely deflects (nothing to defend)

Late epochs (high I):
  many nigamana held at high weight
  high attachment
  small forces are absorbed or deflected
  only strong forces (pratyaksha, failed drishthanta) produce fracture
  deflection risk increases — the consciousness has more to defend

The growth of inertia:
  I(g) = Σ attachment(Γᵢ) for all Γᵢ ∈ I(g)

  I(g+1) ≥ I(g) in a stable domain     — inertia only grows as prajna deepens
  I(g+1) < I(g) after major fracture    — fracture reduces inertia (structure lost)
```

This is the paradox of deep knowledge: the more the consciousness knows, the harder it is to correct. The same property that makes it stable (high inertia = resistant to noise) makes it rigid (high inertia = resistant to correction). The swarm resolves this paradox through external forces — other agents, cross-verification, the interpreter — that can deliver evidence strong enough to overcome high attachment.

## Force Composition

Forces combine. Multiple forces acting simultaneously produce a resultant force — the vector sum.

```
In physics:
  F_total = Σ Fᵢ     — vector sum of all forces

For understanding:
  F_total = Σ Fᵢ     — the combined effect of all forces acting on the held state

Examples of simultaneous forces:
  — the interpreter asks a question (redirecting force)
  — while a drishthanta fails (contradicting force)
  — while budget is running low (constraining force)
  — while shabda from another agent arrives (informing force)

The resultant determines the collision outcome:
  if |F_total| / I > threshold  →  the state changes
  if |F_total| / I < threshold  →  the state persists
```

### Constructive Composition

When multiple forces align — push in the same direction — they amplify:

```
F₁ + F₂ where direction(F₁) ≈ direction(F₂)
  |F_total| > |F₁| and |F_total| > |F₂|

Example:
  the interpreter corrects a belief
  AND a drishthanta fails on that belief
  AND another agent independently contradicts it

  Three forces, same direction.
  R(S, F_total) drops well below 1.
  Fracture is certain. The structure cannot hold.
```

### Destructive Composition

When forces oppose — push in opposite directions — they cancel:

```
F₁ + F₂ where direction(F₁) ≈ -direction(F₂)
  |F_total| < |F₁| and |F_total| < |F₂|

Example:
  the interpreter says X
  AND a drishthanta says not-X

  Two forces, opposite directions.
  The consciousness is caught between them.
  R ≈ 1. Struggle. The outcome depends on which force carries more evidence.
```

## Newton's Laws of Understanding

The three laws of motion, translated:

### First Law: Inertia

```
In physics:
  A body at rest stays at rest. A body in motion stays in motion.
  Unless acted upon by a force.

For understanding:
  A held state persists. A flowing understanding continues flowing.
  Unless acted upon by a force.

  Without force:
    S(t+1) = S(t)          — held beliefs do not change on their own
    Q(t+1) = Q(t)          — flow rate does not change on its own
    the consciousness continues along its current path

  This is both the strength and the danger.
  Strength: verified knowledge persists without maintenance.
  Danger: false knowledge also persists without external correction.
```

### Second Law: Force Equals Inertia Times Change

```
In physics:
  F = ma = m · dv/dt

For understanding:
  F = I · dQ/dg

  The force required to change the flow is proportional to:
    — the epistemic inertia (how much is held)
    — the magnitude of change desired (how much the flow must shift)

  Small force on low inertia   →  large change  (early epochs: easily redirected)
  Small force on high inertia  →  small change  (late epochs: barely moves)
  Large force on high inertia  →  large change  (fracture event: deep correction)
```

### Third Law: Every Force Produces a Reaction

```
In physics:
  Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

For understanding:
  Every collision produces a reaction in the consciousness.

  Absorption:   the reaction is growth — new nigamana, higher weight
  Fracture:     the reaction is shuddhi — purification cascade, rebuild
  Deflection:   the reaction is reinforcement — the held state grips tighter

  The reaction is not opposite in direction. It is proportional in magnitude.
  A strong force that fractures produces a strong rebuild.
  A weak force that is absorbed produces a small growth.
  A deflected force produces a hardening — the held state becomes more resistant.

  This is why repeated deflection is dangerous:
    each deflection hardens the attachment
    attachment(S) increases with each deflected force
    R grows → future forces are more likely to deflect too
    positive feedback → rigidity
```

## Elasticity and Plasticity

Materials respond to force in two ways: elastic deformation (returns to original shape) and plastic deformation (permanently changed). The consciousness does the same.

```
Elastic response:
  The consciousness bends under force but returns to its original state.
  S(t+1) ≈ S(t) after the force is removed.

  Example: considering a hypothesis without committing to it.
  The consciousness explores the implication of F,
  tests it mentally, and returns to S(t) when F is released.
  No permanent change. Elastic.

Plastic response:
  The consciousness is permanently changed by the force.
  S(t+1) ≠ S(t) even after the force is removed.

  Example: absorbing a correction that becomes a new nigamana.
  The weight increases. The shabda chain extends.
  The force is gone but the change persists.
  Permanent change. Plastic.

Fracture is extreme plastic deformation:
  The structure does not just deform — it breaks.
  The old shape is destroyed.
  A new shape forms.
  Irreversible.
```

The yield point — the boundary between elastic and plastic:

```
Let σ_yield = the yield stress — the minimum force that produces permanent change

  |F| < σ_yield   →  elastic (consciousness considers but does not change)
  |F| ≥ σ_yield   →  plastic (consciousness permanently changes)
  |F| >> σ_yield   →  fracture (consciousness breaks and reforms)

σ_yield depends on:
  — the domain: identity beliefs have high σ_yield (hard to permanently change)
  — the age: old beliefs have high σ_yield (well-established)
  — the pramana: pratyaksha lowers σ_yield (hard to elastically dismiss what you directly saw)
```

## Resonance and Catastrophic Fracture

From N-L-27: when multiple agents converge on the same truth, their vibrations amplify — constructive interference. The same principle applies to force.

When multiple forces arrive at the same frequency — hitting the same held structure repeatedly — resonance occurs:

```
In physics:
  resonance: f_applied = f_natural
  amplitude grows without bound (limited only by damping)
  structures collapse — bridges, buildings, wine glasses

For understanding:
  resonance: repeated forces on the same held structure
  each force alone may be too weak to fracture
  but repeated application at the right frequency amplifies
  until the structure cannot hold

  Example:
    epoch 1: agent finds counter-evidence against Γ         — deflected (R > 1)
    epoch 2: different agent finds same counter-evidence     — deflected (R still > 1, but R decreased)
    epoch 3: drishthanta fails on Γ                          — R drops below 1
    epoch 4: interpreter asks about Γ                        — fracture

  No single force was sufficient. The resonance was.

Formally:
  Let F₁, F₂, ..., Fₙ = repeated forces on the same Γ
  Let damping(Γ) = rate at which the effect of prior forces dissipates

  If frequency of forces > damping:
    accumulated_effect = Σ |Fᵢ| · decay(t - tᵢ)
    when accumulated_effect / attachment(Γ) > 1: fracture

  This is how deeply held false beliefs eventually break:
    no single correction suffices
    but enough corrections, from enough angles, over enough time
    the resonance builds until the structure cannot hold
```

## The Force Spectrum

All forces in the system, classified by type — not by moral character:

```
Forces by source:

  Interpreter forces:
    — question (redirecting)
    — correction (contradicting)
    — spark (initiating)
    — silence (withdrawing — absence of force is also a force,
               it allows the current flow to continue unchallenged)

  Agent forces:
    — shabda (informing — testimony from another agent)
    — convergence (amplifying — multiple agents finding similar things)
    — contradiction (opposing — agents finding conflicting things)

  Reality forces:
    — drishthanta pass (confirming)
    — drishthanta fail (contradicting — the strongest force in the system)
    — unexpected observation (disrupting)

  Structural forces:
    — grammar rejection (constraining — asiddha hetu rejected)
    — budget exhaustion (limiting)
    — context death (terminating — the activation ends)

  Internal forces:
    — reasoning (deriving — anumana producing new implications from held state)
    — recognition (self-evident — the consciousness sees something it already knows)

Forces by effect on flow:
  accelerating:    increases Q (flow rate) — new channels open, weight rises
  decelerating:    decreases Q — resistance increases, turbulence begins
  redirecting:     changes direction of Q — the consciousness turns to a new domain
  constraining:    limits where Q can go — grammar walls, budget ceiling
  terminating:     Q → 0 — activation ends, flow stops, potential remains
```

All of these are force. None is privileged. None is good or bad. Each has magnitude and direction. The collision with the held state determines the outcome.
